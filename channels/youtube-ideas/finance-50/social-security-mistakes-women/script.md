## MISTAKE 1 — Claiming too early at 62 (0:35–1:45)
[ON-SCREEN: "Age 62 = ~30% smaller check. Forever."]

The most common mistake is grabbing benefits the moment you turn 62. It feels safe. But if your full retirement age is 67 — which it is for everyone born in 1960 or later — claiming at 62 permanently cuts your monthly check by about 30%.

That's not a one-time cut. It's every check, for the rest of your life. And because women live longer on average, that smaller check has more years to add up against you.

[ON-SCREEN: "Wait to 70 = +24% bigger check"]

Flip it around: for every year you wait past full retirement age, Social Security adds 8% — up to age 70. Wait from 67 to 70 and your benefit grows by 24%. If you're in good health and can cover the gap, waiting is often the most powerful raise you'll ever get.

*Source: SSA Publication 05-10035; SSA Delayed Retirement Credits.*

## MISTAKE 2 — Overlooking the survivor benefit (1:45–3:00)
[ON-SCREEN: "The widow keeps the BIGGER check — not both"]

This one is huge for married women, and almost nobody plans for it. When one spouse passes away, the survivor doesn't keep both checks. She keeps the higher of the two — and the smaller one goes away.

Here's why that matters: if your husband was the higher earner, the size of his benefit becomes your income for the rest of your life. And if he delayed his benefit to age 70, those delay credits carry over to you as the survivor.

[ON-SCREEN: "His delay = your raise, for life"]

So when the higher earner waits, he's not just boosting his own check — he's protecting his wife with a larger benefit for the years she may spend on her own. Survivor benefits can start as early as age 60, but claiming that early reduces them, so the timing deserves real thought.

*Source: SSA Survivor Benefits; ssa.tools Delayed Retirement Credits.*

## MISTAKE 3 — Not knowing the divorced-spouse rule (3:00–4:00)
[ON-SCREEN: "Married 10+ years? You may qualify on his record"]

If you were married for at least 10 years and you're currently unmarried, you may be able to claim benefits on your ex-spouse's record — even if he has remarried.

And here's the relief: it does not reduce his benefit, it doesn't affect his current spouse, and he doesn't even need to be notified. If you've been divorced for more than two years, you can often claim even if he hasn't started his own benefits yet.

So many women leave this money on the table simply because no one told them it existed. If this is you, it's worth a call to Social Security.

*Source: SSA Spouse's Benefits blog (2024-07-11).*

## MISTAKE 4 — Misunderstanding the spousal benefit (4:00–5:00)
[ON-SCREEN: "Spousal max = 50% of his FRA amount — not his age-70 amount"]

A spousal benefit can be up to half of your spouse's full-retirement-age benefit — but two details trip women up.

First: it's 50% of his benefit at full retirement age, not his bigger age-70 amount. His delay credits don't increase your spousal benefit while you're both living.

[ON-SCREEN: "You get the higher of the two — not both"]

Second: you don't get your own benefit plus the spousal benefit. Social Security pays your own first, then tops you up to the higher amount. And if you claim before your own full retirement age, that spousal benefit is permanently reduced too.

*Source: SSA Spouse's Benefits; Greenbush Financial spousal guide (2026).*

## MISTAKE 5 — Missing Medicare at 65 (5:00–6:00)
[ON-SCREEN: "Delaying Social Security ≠ delaying Medicare"]

Even if you're waiting until 70 to claim Social Security, your Medicare clock still starts at 65. These are two separate decisions.

If you don't sign up for Medicare around your 65th birthday — and you don't have qualifying coverage through a job — you can face late-enrollment penalties that raise your premiums for the rest of your life.

[ON-SCREEN: "Sign up ~3 months before your 65th birthday"]

The fix is simple: mark age 65 on your calendar, and enroll in the window around your birthday, even if your Social Security check is still years away.

*Source: SSA Publication 05-10035; SSA Delayed Retirement Credits.*
